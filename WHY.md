# Why bother?

You already have a font. Every device hands you one for free, and it works —
the words arrive, the reader reads them, the message lands. So why bother
choosing another?

Because the reader judges the envelope before they open the letter. A résumé in
the system default and the same résumé in a face someone actually chose say two
different things before a single word is read. One says *here is my experience*.
The other says that — and, underneath it, *I thought about how to reach you.*

**Your font is your signature.** You sign every message twice: once with what it
says, once with how it looks. The second signature lands first — beneath thought,
in the half-second before reading begins. A wedding invite in a thin nervous
script tells you the couple is gentle. A notice in cut-out capitals puts you on
edge. The letters did that, not the words. The shape the words were wearing.

The default is not neutral. It only pretends to be. Taking whatever the machine
offered is still a choice; it just says *I didn't make one.* And indifference is
legible — the reader feels it as a draft under the door.

And the usual way of choosing is scattered. Normally you go hunting across a
dozen websites — one to browse, another to type your own words into a preview, a
third to read the license, a fourth to finally download the file — comparing
faces from memory because no two of them ever sit on the same screen. You open
twenty tabs to choose one font.

That's the whole reason this exists. Camouflage keeps the entire shelf in one
place: every face, your text, side by side, right now. Tell it what you're
making and it hands you the one that fits. Type a single word and watch the whole
library wear it. No tab-hopping, no specialist's afternoon of pairing and
licensing and dread — you don't have to become a typographer. You just have to
stop signing in a hand that isn't yours.

So — bother. Not to decorate. To be understood, and to sound like yourself while
you are.

> Your words say what you mean. Your typeface says whether to believe you.
